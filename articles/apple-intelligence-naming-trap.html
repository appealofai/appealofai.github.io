Apple Intelligence still has a naming problem.

Apple did not just announce features. It named an expectation, which makes every Siri delay and ordinary assistant failure feel larger than a normal product gap.

Contents The claim What is fair The appeal Sources

The claim

I do not think Apple's problem is that it failed to say the magic AI words. It may be the opposite. "Apple Intelligence" is a beautiful name, but it is also a huge promise. It makes the product sound inevitable before the experience feels inevitable.

The trap is simple: Apple usually wins by making technology feel calm. AI still feels unsettled. If Siri misses ordinary requests, the miss becomes evidence against the brand name.

What is fair

The careful version is this: Apple did introduce Apple Intelligence as a privacy-centered AI system, and recent WWDC coverage keeps showing how much the company wants AI to feel integrated rather than theatrical. Expectations around Siri have also been strained by delays and reporting about internal pressure.

What should not be overstated is the anecdotal part. "Siri is often wrong" is a reasonable user experience claim, but it is not a clean universal fact. The stronger argument is product-language debt: when you call the layer "Intelligence," the tolerance for ordinary assistant weirdness collapses.

The Appeal

  • Apple still has the best chance to make AI feel private and ordinary.
  • The risk is a promise arriving before the feeling.
  • The product needs less naming gravity and more quiet competence.
